Understanding the Risks to Trees During Construction

Construction sites pose numerous threats to trees. The most obvious is direct physical damage from machinery. Heavy equipment can easily scrape bark, break branches, or even topple entire trees. Soil compaction is another significant risk. The weight of equipment and materials compresses the soil, reducing its ability to hold water and air, which are essential for root health. This compaction may also restrict root growth, making it difficult for the tree to absorb nutrients. Root damage is often unseen, but it is one of the most damaging consequences of construction. Excavation for foundations, utilities, or landscaping can sever roots, weakening the tree and making it susceptible to disease and insect infestations. Changes to drainage patterns can also harm trees. Construction can alter the way water flows across a site, leading to either excessive flooding or drought conditions, both of which can stress trees. Finally, the storage of materials, such as concrete or chemicals, near trees can contaminate the soil and harm the roots.

Pre-Construction Planning and Tree Inventory

The first step in protecting trees during construction is a thorough pre-construction planning process that includes a detailed tree inventory. Hire a certified arborist to assess the health, species, and size of all trees on and near the construction location. This inventory should identify trees that are particularly valuable or vulnerable. The arborist can then develop a tree protection plan that outlines specific measures to minimize the impact of construction activities. This plan should clearly define tree protection zones, which are areas around trees that are off-limits to construction equipment and activities. The plan should also specify how to prune roots or branches that may be affected by construction, and it should outline a monitoring schedule to ensure that the protection measures are being followed. A construction plan that considers existing trees from the outset saves money and improves community relations. In Abilene, it is often a requirement to protect significant trees.

Implementing Tree Protection Measures

Once the tree protection plan is in place, it's crucial to implement the recommended measures. Physical barriers are essential for protecting trees from direct damage. These barriers should be sturdy and highly visible, such as chain-link fences, and should be placed at the edge of the tree protection zone. The barriers should be high enough to prevent equipment from accidentally hitting the trees. Mulching around trees within the protection zone can help to reduce soil compaction and retain moisture. A layer of organic mulch, such as wood chips, should be applied to a depth of 4-6 inches. Avoid piling mulch directly against the tree trunk, as this can create conditions that promote rot. Root pruning may be necessary if excavation is required near trees. A certified arborist should perform the root pruning to minimize damage to the tree. The roots should be cut cleanly with a sharp saw, and the cut ends should be treated with a sealant to prevent disease. Proper watering and fertilization can help trees withstand the stress of construction. Regular watering is especially important during dry periods, and fertilization can provide trees with the nutrients they need to stay healthy. However, it's important to avoid over-fertilizing, as this can also stress trees. It’s important to consult with an arborist to come up with a proper plan.

Managing Soil Compaction and Drainage

Soil compaction is a common problem on construction sites, but there are several steps you can take to minimize its impact on trees. Avoid driving heavy equipment over the root zones of trees. If equipment must be driven near trees, use lightweight equipment or spread the load with plywood or other materials. Aerating the soil can help to improve its structure and drainage. This can be done with a soil aerator or by hand with a garden fork. Be careful not to damage roots when aerating the soil. Improving drainage can also help to protect trees from water damage. This can be done by installing drainage pipes or by grading the soil to direct water away from trees. It's also important to avoid piling soil or other materials against the trunks of trees, as this can trap moisture and promote rot. Post-Construction Tree Care

Even with the best planning and protection measures, construction can still stress trees. Post-construction care is essential for helping trees recover and thrive. Continue watering trees regularly, especially during dry periods. Monitor trees for signs of stress, such as leaf browning, wilting, or dieback. If you notice any problems, consult with a certified arborist. Fertilize trees as needed, following the recommendations of an arborist. Prune any dead, damaged, or diseased branches. This will help to improve the tree's overall health and appearance. Protect trees from pests and diseases. Monitor trees for signs of infestation or disease, and take appropriate action if necessary.
